## 1. The four search intents (and why they shape everything)

Before targeting any keyword, classify its intent. Targeting a keyword without matching its intent guarantees underperformance.

| Intent | Examples | Page type that ranks |
|--------|----------|---------------------|
| **Informational** | "what is a hardtail bike", "how to fix a flat tire" | Guides, how-tos, definitions, blog posts |
| **Navigational** | "trek website", "facebook login" | The official destination only |
| **Commercial / investigation** | "best mountain bikes 2026", "trek vs giant", "marlin 7 review" | Comparison guides, reviews, listicles |
| **Transactional** | "buy trek marlin 7", "mountain bike sale", "bike shop near me" | Product pages, category pages, local listings |

The same keyword can shift intent over time — Google watches user behavior and adjusts. Always check the **current top 10 SERP** for a query to confirm what type of page Google is rewarding right now. This is more reliable than any tool's "intent classification."

## 2. Seed keywords and expansion

### Step 1: Generate seed keywords

Start with 10–20 broad terms central to the business. For a Freiburg bike shop:

- mountain bike, road bike, e-bike, gravel bike, kids bike (categories)
- bike repair, bike fitting, bike rental (services)
- bike helmet, bike lights, bike lock, bike clothing (accessories)
- Freiburg bike shop, bike store Freiburg (local)

### Step 2: Expand each seed

For each seed, generate variants:

- **Modifiers**: best, cheap, top, premium, beginner, professional
- **Buyer-stage qualifiers**: how to choose, buying guide, vs, review, near me, sale
- **Specifications**: under €1000, for tall riders, electric, women's
- **Long-tail completions**: "best mountain bike for beginners under 1500 euros"

Methods to expand without paid tools:

- **Google autocomplete** — type the seed and note what Google suggests. Append letters a–z one by one ("mountain bike a", "mountain bike b", etc.) for full coverage.
- **People Also Ask** — every PAA box on a SERP is a research-stage query opportunity.
- **Related searches** at the bottom of SERPs.
- **YouTube autocomplete** — surfaces video-intent keywords that may not appear in Google's text autocomplete.
- **Reddit & forums** — niche communities (r/bicycling, German biking forums) reveal exact language users use.
- **Amazon search bar** — for product keywords, Amazon's autocomplete reflects buying intent specifically.

## 3. Long-tail strategy

A "long-tail" keyword is a longer, more specific query with lower individual search volume but higher conversion intent. Examples:

- Head: "mountain bike" (50,000 searches/mo, hyper-competitive)
- Mid: "beginner mountain bike" (3,000 searches/mo, competitive)
- Long-tail: "best mountain bike for tall beginner under 1500 euros" (90 searches/mo, much easier to rank)

**Long-tail wins for small sites** because:
- Lower competition — often dominated by content that hasn't been optimized
- Higher intent — users who type long queries usually know what they want
- Easier to write content that genuinely matches intent

**The strategy**: rather than fight for one head term, build a cluster of 20–50 long-tail pages around it. Each captures a slice of the audience. Together, they often beat the head term in total traffic and conversion.

## 4. Keyword difficulty — without paid tools

Paid SEO tools (Ahrefs, Semrush, Moz) sell a "Keyword Difficulty" score. Without them, judge difficulty using these signals from the SERP itself:

### Inspect the top 10 organic results for the query and note:

1. **Domain types** — are the top results all huge brands and major publications? Hard. Are they small blogs and forums? Easier.
2. **Content quality** — read the top 3. If they're shallow, you can win with deeper content. If they're masterworks, hard.
3. **Content age** — is the top result 5 years old? Freshness alone might win. Are they all updated this year? Harder.
4. **SERP features** — featured snippet, AI Overview, shopping carousels, video results, ads. Each absorbs clicks. The more SERP features, the less click-share is left for organic.
5. **Domain authority of competitors** — even without a tool, you can eyeball: nytimes.com vs randomblog.com is obvious.
6. **Number of words / depth** — if every top result is 4000+ words, that's the bar.

### Quick categories

- **Easy:** none of the top 10 are major brands; SERP has few features; top results are thin
- **Medium:** mix of small and large sites; some SERP features; top results are competent but not exceptional
- **Hard:** dominated by authoritative sites; many SERP features; top results are deep and well-maintained
- **Don't bother:** all top 10 are massive brands, AI Overview answers the query, and the topic is generic

For a small/local site, **target Easy and Medium**. Build authority over time before fighting Hard keywords.

## 5. Competitor keyword gap analysis

Goal: find keywords competitors rank for that you don't.

### Without paid tools

1. Pick 3–5 direct competitors (not industry giants — pick sites at your scale plus one or two slightly above).
2. For each competitor, use `site:competitor.com` searches to find their content. Browse their blog/category pages to inventory topics.
3. Check their top-performing content with free tools (e.g., similar-traffic-estimator browser extensions, social-share counts).
4. List the topics they cover that you don't.
5. Filter by:
   - Topical fit with your business
   - Your ability to write a genuinely better page
   - Long-tail variant feasibility

### With paid tools (briefly)

Ahrefs, Semrush, and Sistrix all have keyword-gap features that will return a programmatic list of "they rank, you don't" keywords. If the user has access, this saves hours.

### Output format

```
Top 10 keyword gaps to target this quarter:

1. "fahrrad-leasing" — Competitor X ranks #5, est. 1900/mo, German market
   Why: We offer leasing too but have no page on it
   Effort: Medium (write 1500-word guide page)

2. ...
```

## 6. Free tools that work in 2026

These are all genuinely useful at zero cost:

### Volume / data
- **Google Search Console** — your own historical query data is the best keyword research input you have
- **Google Trends** — relative volume over time, regional differences, seasonality
- **Google Keyword Planner** — requires a Google Ads account (free); volumes are bucketed but directionally useful
- **AnswerThePublic** (limited free queries) — generates question-format keywords
- **AlsoAsked** (limited free queries) — generates People Also Ask trees
- **Keyword Surfer** (browser extension) — overlays volume estimates on Google SERPs
- **Ubersuggest** (limited free queries) — keyword suggestions and basic difficulty

### Intent / SERP analysis
- **The actual Google SERP** — read the top 10 results manually
- **People Also Ask box** — surfaces related questions
- **Related searches** at the SERP bottom

### Local-specific
- **Google Business Profile insights** — tells you what queries triggered impressions for your profile
- **Google Maps autocomplete** — surfaces local-intent keywords

## 7. Mapping keywords to pages

Each target keyword must map to **exactly one** page on the site. This avoids cannibalization (covered in `references/gsc-analysis.md`).

### Mapping rules

- **One primary keyword per page.** The H1, title, and URL should reflect this primary keyword.
- **Secondary/related keywords on the same page.** A page targeting "best mountain bike under 1500" can also rank for "affordable mountain bike", "cheap mountain bike under 1500" — these are intent-equivalent variants. Don't make a separate page for each.
- **Distinct pages for distinct intents.** "Mountain bike" (broad commercial) and "buy mountain bike Freiburg" (transactional + local) need separate pages.

### Output format

A keyword map is typically a spreadsheet or table:

| Primary KW | Search vol | Intent | Target URL | Page status |
|-----------|-----------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| best mountain bike beginner | 1,200 | Commercial | /guides/best-mtb-beginner | Doesn't exist — write |
| trek marlin 7 review | 320 | Commercial | /products/trek-marlin-7 | Add review section |
| ... | | | | |

## 8. Local-keyword research specifics

For local businesses, augment standard keyword research with:

### "Near me" and city/neighborhood variants

- `[service] near me` — Google rewrites this to `[service] in [user location]` server-side, but it's still searched as-is by users
- `[service] in [city]` — explicit local intent
- `[service] [neighborhood]` — hyperlocal, less competition (e.g., "Fahrradladen Wiehre" vs "Fahrradladen Freiburg")
- `[service] [city center / Innenstadt / downtown]` — relevant for tourists / commuters

### Service-specific local keywords

- `[service] open now` / `[service] open Sunday` — convenience-driven
- `[service] same-day` / `[service] emergency` — urgency-driven
- `cheap [service] [city]` / `affordable [service] [city]` — price-sensitive

### Modifier keywords

- `best [category] shop in [city]`
- `top-rated [service] [city]`
- `[city] [service] reviews`

### Local-content keywords (for content-marketing pages)

- `things to do in [city]` (if relevant — e.g., bike rental could capture tourism queries)
- `[activity] in [city]` (e.g., "mountain biking in Freiburg")
- `[seasonal event] [city]` (e.g., "Velo Freiburg Messe 2026")

## 9. Common keyword research mistakes

- **Chasing volume over intent.** A 10,000/month keyword with mismatched intent converts at 0.1%. A 200/month keyword with perfect intent converts at 8%. The latter is worth more.
- **Ignoring zero-volume keywords.** Tools report zero for many real, low-volume queries. Long-tail and hyper-specific queries often show zero but exist and convert. GSC reveals them.
- **Targeting only English variants for a multi-language audience.** Each language is its own keyword research project.
- **Listing keywords without mapping them to pages.** A list with no page strategy is just a list. Always end keyword research with a mapping table.
- **Overestimating own ability.** "We can rank for [head term]" — usually wrong without years of authority-building. Start long-tail.
- **Not revisiting research.** Search behavior shifts. Re-run keyword research at least annually, ideally quarterly for fast-moving niches.

## 1. The local SEO model (organic + map pack)

When users search a local-intent query (e.g., "bike shop Freiburg" or "bike shop near me"), Google can show **two ranking systems** on the same SERP:

- **Map pack / Local pack** — usually 3 results with map, names, ratings, hours. Driven by Google Business Profile (GBP) signals.
- **Organic results** — the standard 10 blue links. Driven by traditional SEO signals.

A complete local strategy targets both. They're related but not identical: a business with strong organic SEO might still be invisible in the map pack if its GBP is poorly maintained, and vice versa.

## 2. Google Business Profile optimization

GBP (formerly Google My Business) is the single highest-leverage local SEO tool. **Every recommendation in this section assumes the user has claimed and verified their GBP.** If they haven't, that's step zero.

### Profile completeness

- **Name** — exactly the business's real name. **Don't keyword-stuff** ("Bikehaus Freiburg | Cheap Bikes & Repairs Near You" violates GBP guidelines and risks suspension). The name field is sacred.
- **Primary category** — the single most important category. Choose the most specific match, not the most general. "Bicycle Shop" beats "Sporting Goods Store" for a bike retailer.
- **Secondary categories** — add up to 9 more if they truly apply. "Bicycle Repair Shop" makes sense for a shop that does both. Don't add unrelated categories to "rank for more things" — Google can detect this.
- **Description** — 750 characters. Use it for natural language about what the business does, who it serves, and what makes it different. Keywords help, but write for humans first.
- **Hours** — must be accurate. Including special holiday hours, which Google will prompt about.
- **Service area** — for service-area businesses (mobile mechanics, etc.), define it precisely. Don't claim an unrealistically large area.
- **Attributes** — fill in every relevant attribute (wheelchair accessible, accepts credit cards, has Wi-Fi, etc.). These power filtering in Maps and add SERP signals.
- **Photos** — minimum 10, ideally 30+. Mix of: storefront exterior, interior, products/services, team. Photos drive both visibility and conversion.
- **Products / Services** — list each major product/service with descriptions and photos. These appear in the GBP panel and feed local pack relevance.
- **Posts** — weekly Google Posts (events, offers, updates) signal active management to Google.

### Special features by business type

- **Restaurants:** menus (preferably structured), online ordering links, popular dishes
- **Retail:** product feeds, "what people are saying" highlights
- **Services:** booking/quote buttons, response time

### GBP performance metrics

GBP has its own analytics ("Performance" tab in the dashboard). Track:
- Searches that triggered the profile (direct vs. discovery vs. branded)
- Calls, direction requests, website clicks
- Photo views
- Posts and Q&A engagement

These complement GSC and tell you what's happening in Maps specifically.

## 3. NAP consistency

NAP = **Name, Address, Phone**. Every place online your business appears, NAP must be **byte-identical**. Inconsistencies confuse Google about whether two listings are the same business.

**Audit:**
- Pick the canonical NAP (use exactly what's on the GBP profile)
- Search the business name in Google and check every result on the first 5 pages
- Note any variations: "St." vs "Street", "GmbH" vs no suffix, different phone formatting
- Use a citation-management tool (or do it manually) to align everything

For new businesses, decide once on the canonical NAP and use it everywhere from day one.

## 4. Local citations

A citation = any mention of the business's NAP on another website, even without a link. Citations reinforce Google's understanding that the business exists, where it is, and what it does.

### Tier 1 (essential, get these first)

- Google Business Profile
- Bing Places for Business
- Apple Maps Connect
- Facebook Business Page
- Instagram for Business

### Tier 2 (industry- and country-specific)

- For Germany: Gelbe Seiten, Das Örtliche, 11880, GoYellow, Cylex, Yelp.de
- For UK: Yell, Thomson Local
- For US: Yelp, Yellow Pages, BBB, Foursquare
- Industry-specific directories (e.g., for bike shops: cycling-club directories, manufacturer dealer locators, route-planning apps that list services)

### Tier 3 (long-tail)

Local business associations, chamber of commerce, neighborhood blogs, event sponsorship pages, local press mentions. These add diversity to the citation profile.

**Quality > quantity.** Ten high-authority, fully-completed citations beat fifty thin auto-submitted ones.

## 5. Reviews strategy

Reviews are a top-3 local pack ranking factor. They also drive conversion massively — a 4.5★ business gets ~2x more clicks than a 3.5★ one in the local pack.

### Acquisition

- **Ask** every satisfied customer at the right moment (after a purchase, after the bike fitting, etc.).
- **Make it easy** — share a direct GBP review link via SMS/email/QR code. The link format is `https://g.page/r/{place-id}/review` (Google generates this for each profile).
- **Don't** offer incentives for reviews. Both Google and many platforms ban this and detect it.
- **Don't** ask in batches all at once. A sudden spike of 50 reviews looks suspicious and can trigger filters.

### Response

- Respond to **every review**, positive and negative.
- For positive reviews: brief, warm, specific (mention something from the review). Not a copy-paste template.
- For negative reviews: acknowledge, take responsibility where appropriate, offer to make it right offline. **Never argue publicly** — the response is read by future customers, not just the reviewer.
- Response time matters; aim for 48 hours.

### Volume and freshness

Google looks at both total review count and recent review velocity. A business with 800 reviews but none in the last year looks dormant. Aim for a steady trickle of new reviews ongoing.

### Other platforms

Don't only optimize Google reviews. Yelp, Tripadvisor (if relevant), Trustpilot, and industry-specific platforms all influence reputation and sometimes appear in SERPs themselves.

## 6. Local content strategy

Beyond the homepage, local SEO benefits from content tied to the geography:

### Location pages (for multi-location businesses)
- One unique page per location, with that location's NAP, hours, photos, team, embedded map, and unique copy (don't template).

### City / neighborhood pages
- For service businesses: "Bike repair in Freiburg-Wiehre", "Bike repair in Freiburg-Herdern". Each genuinely different and relevant — not just templates with the city name swapped.

### Local guides and content
- "Best mountain bike trails near Freiburg"
- "Where to bike in the Black Forest: 12 routes ranked by difficulty"
- "Cycling commute routes through Freiburg city center"

These earn local backlinks (from tourism sites, local blogs), drive local-intent traffic, and build topical authority for the area.

### Local-intent keywords

Find them by:
- Looking at GSC for queries containing the city/neighborhood/region
- Google autocomplete: type "[service] in [city]" and note the suggestions
- "People also ask" boxes for local queries
- Competitors' meta tags and H1s

## 7. Local link building

Backlinks remain a major ranking factor. For local businesses, prioritize **locally relevant links**:

- **Local press / blogs** — sponsor or be quoted in a story
- **Local chambers of commerce / business associations** — usually offer a member directory listing
- **Local events you sponsor** — get a backlink from the event page
- **Charities / nonprofits** — partner and get a "supporters" page link
- **Suppliers / manufacturers** — many maintain dealer locators that link back
- **Customer interviews / case studies on partner sites**

A handful of local, contextual links is worth more than dozens of generic directory links.

## 8. Map pack ranking factors

Google's local algorithm considers (roughly in order of weight):

1. **Relevance** — does the business match the query? (Categories, name, services listed)
2. **Distance** — how close is the business to the searcher? (For "near me" queries especially.)
3. **Prominence** — how well-known is the business? (Reviews, citations, organic SEO of the website, brand search volume, mentions in news.)

The first two are largely fixed (you can't move your business). **Prominence is where you compete.** The strategies in sections 4–7 above all build prominence.

Note that distance is measured from the searcher's location, not the city center. A bike shop in Freiburg-Wiehre wins for users in Wiehre but loses to a Herdern shop for users in Herdern, all else equal. This is why neighborhood pages matter.

## 9. Multi-location handling

For chains or multi-location businesses:

- **One GBP per location**, each fully managed
- **One unique location page per location** on the website, linked from a "Locations" hub
- **Consistent NAP per location**, distinct from other locations (no shared phone numbers across locations if avoidable)
- **Schema markup** with separate `LocalBusiness` JSON-LD per location page
- **Reviews segmented per location** — don't aggregate across the brand

## 10. Common local SEO mistakes

- **Keyword-stuffing the GBP business name.** Highest-frequency violation. Risks suspension.
- **Using a virtual office / mailbox address.** Google's verification process catches this and the listing won't rank.
- **Inconsistent NAP across the web.** A spreadsheet of citations and their current state pays off.
- **Ignoring Google Q&A.** Anyone can ask and answer questions on a GBP. Monitor and respond officially, or competitors / random users will define your business.
- **Treating GBP as set-and-forget.** Google rewards active management. Posts, photos, Q&A responses, review responses — ongoing.
- **Building location pages that are 95% the same template.** Google calls this "doorway pages" and will deprioritize. Each location page must be genuinely useful.
- **Not capturing Maps clicks in analytics.** GBP's website-click traffic is tracked separately. Use UTM parameters on the GBP website link to see this in your analytics.
